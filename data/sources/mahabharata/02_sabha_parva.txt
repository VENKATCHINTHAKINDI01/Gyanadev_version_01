1. After the Pandavas had established their magnificent kingdom of Indraprastha, Yudhishthira decided to perform the Rajasuya sacrifice, the great sacrifice performed by a king who wishes to be recognized as the lord of all kings.
2. For this sacrifice to be valid, all the kings of the earth had to either submit to Yudhishthira's sovereignty or be conquered by him. Arjuna and the other brothers were sent in all directions to bring the kings under Yudhishthira's rule.
3. Arjuna went north and conquered all the kingdoms in that direction. Bhima went east, Nakula went west, and Sahadeva went south. All returned having brought the kings under the sovereignty of Yudhishthira.
4. The Rajasuya sacrifice was performed with great pomp and ceremony. Kings and sages from all parts of the world came to attend, bringing rich gifts.
5. At the ceremony, it was decided that Krishna should receive the first honor. This was opposed by Shishupala, the king of Chedi, who was an enemy of Krishna.
6. Shishupala began to abuse Krishna with terrible insults, recounting what he considered to be Krishna's crimes. Krishna had promised Shishupala's mother that he would forgive her son a hundred transgressions.
7. When Shishupala had committed his hundredth sin, Krishna, with a single discus, cut off his head. The assembled kings were struck with awe.
8. After the Rajasuya, Duryodhana stayed on in Indraprastha and was shown the great palace of illusions built by the divine architect Maya. He saw its wonders with growing envy and jealousy.
9. When Duryodhana mistook the crystal floor for water and lifted his robes, then fell into the actual pool of water thinking it was a crystal floor, Bhima and Arjuna laughed, and Draupadi laughed most of all.
10. This laughter wounded Duryodhana deeply and he returned to Hastinapura burning with the desire for revenge. He could not eat or sleep, consumed by envy of the Pandavas' wealth and happiness.
11. Shakuni, his maternal uncle, proposed the scheme of the dice game. He told Duryodhana: Challenge Yudhishthira to a game of dice. I will play on your behalf and I never lose.
12. Duryodhana went to his father Dhritarashtra and begged him to summon the Pandavas for a game of dice. Though Dhritarashtra knew it was wrong, blinded by love for his son, he agreed.
13. The Pandavas were summoned to Hastinapura for the dice game. The wise Vidura, the half-brother of Dhritarashtra, warned the king that this would lead to disaster, but Dhritarashtra would not listen.
14. Yudhishthira knew that the dice were loaded and that Shakuni was a cheat, yet as a kshatriya he felt bound to accept the challenge. He told himself that he would not stake more than he could afford to lose.
15. The game began, and from the very first throw Yudhishthira lost. He staked his treasury and lost. He staked his chariots and horses and lost. He staked his army and lost.
16. Yudhishthira kept gambling, thinking each time that the next throw would restore his losses. But Shakuni with his loaded dice always won.
17. Finally Yudhishthira staked his brothers one by one — Nakula, Sahadeva, Arjuna, Bhima — and lost each one.
18. Then in a fever of desperation, Yudhishthira staked himself and lost. Finally he staked Draupadi, their beloved wife, and lost her too.
19. Duryodhana immediately sent Vidura to bring Draupadi to the assembly hall to serve as a slave. But Vidura refused, saying this was against all dharma.
20. Then Duryodhana sent his brother Duhshasana to fetch Draupadi. Duhshasana found Draupadi and dragged her by the hair to the assembly hall.
21. Draupadi, dressed in a single garment for she was in her monthly period, was dragged before the assembled kings, Pandavas, and elders.
22. Draupadi asked: Was my husband already a slave when he staked me? If he had already lost himself, how could he have the right to stake me?
23. No one in the assembly could answer this question. The great grandfather Bhishma himself said: The question of Draupadi is a subtle one and I cannot give a clear answer.
24. Karna laughed and told Duhshasana to disrobe Draupadi in the assembly. Duhshasana seized her garment and began to pull.
25. Draupadi cried out in her heart to Krishna: O Govinda, O dweller in Dwarka, do you not see my dishonor? O Madhusudana, O protector of the distressed, save me!
26. Though Krishna was far away in Dwarka, he heard Draupadi's prayer and by his divine power caused an endless stream of cloth to appear as Duhshasana pulled.
27. For a long time Duhshasana pulled and pulled but could not reach the end of the cloth. Finally he collapsed exhausted, and a great mountain of cloth lay around Draupadi.
28. Bhima swore a terrible oath: I shall one day tear open the chest of Duhshasana and drink his blood, or I shall never enter the world of my ancestors.
29. Arjuna swore that he would kill Karna in battle. Yudhishthira remained silent in his grief and shame.
30. The wise Vidura predicted that this day's events would lead to the destruction of the Kuru race. Evil omens appeared throughout the kingdom.
31. Dhritarashtra, frightened by the omens and moved by pity, called Draupadi to him and offered her three boons.
32. Draupadi asked first for the freedom of Yudhishthira, then for the freedom of all her other husbands along with their weapons and chariots. With these three boons, the slavery of the Pandavas was ended.
33. But Duryodhana immediately demanded another game of dice. The stake this time was that the losers must go into the forest for twelve years and spend a thirteenth year in disguise. If recognized during the thirteenth year, another twelve years of exile would begin.
34. Again Yudhishthira accepted the challenge, and again Shakuni won. The Pandavas prepared to go into exile in the forest.
35. As the Pandavas departed, Draupadi's eldest son Dhrishtadyumna told her not to weep, for one day justice would be done and the insult of this day would be avenged.
36. The Pandavas walked out of the city with their heads bowed, dressed in deerskin as hermits go. The people of Hastinapura wept to see their beloved princes humbled so.
37. Only Vidura spoke words of comfort to the departing Pandavas, reminding them that their righteousness was their strength and that truth would ultimately prevail.
38. Dhritarashtra, when he heard that the Pandavas had left the city, felt a moment of regret and asked Vidura whether he had done right. Vidura replied: What has been done cannot be undone, but perhaps disaster can still be averted if you act wisely now.
39. Duryodhana and his friends celebrated the departure of the Pandavas and believed that their troubles were over forever. But wiser men around them knew that the seeds of a terrible war had been sown that day.
40. Thus ended the Sabha Parva, the book of the assembly, with the great humiliation of the Pandavas and the beginning of the events that would lead to the great war of Kurukshetra.
